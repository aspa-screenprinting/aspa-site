Plastisol vs Water-Based Ink: When to Use Each

Updated March 2026 · 18 min read · By ASPA Editorial Team

Screen printing inks fall into two main camps, and your choice affects everything: feel, durability, setup, cleanup, and what you can print on. This comprehensive guide walks you through both so you can choose wisely—or use both strategically to serve more clients and scale your shop.

Two Inks, Two Philosophies

Screen printing ink choices come down to two fundamentally different approaches to color deposition and fabric interaction. Plastisol ink sits on top of the fabric like a discrete layer. Water-based ink soaks into the fabric fibers, becoming part of the garment itself. Neither is inherently "better"—they serve different purposes and different market segments.

For decades, plastisol dominated the industry. It's forgiving, reliable, widely available in virtually every color imaginable, and works on any fabric. Water-based inks were the fringe player—messier, harder to control, but producing a softer hand-feel. Over the last 10 years, that dynamic has shifted. Fashion and apparel brands increasingly demand water-based, sustainability-minded shops are investing heavily in water-based systems, and discharge printing has become a premium service offering. Today's successful screen printing shops understand both.

This guide breaks down the science, economics, and practical realities of both inks. You'll understand when each excels, where they fall short, and how to make decisions that align with your business model, client base, and growth strategy.

What is Plastisol Ink?

Plastisol is a suspension of polyvinyl chloride (PVC) particles in a plasticizer—a chemical that keeps the PVC soft and workable at room temperature. Think of it as tiny plastic beads suspended in oil. Unlike water or other solvents, plastisol never air-dries. It sits in your screen indefinitely, ready to print again. The ink stays on top of the fabric like a thin plastic film.

Plastisol requires heat to cure. When exposed to 320°F (160°C), the PVC particles fuse together, creating a durable, flexible coating bonded to the fabric. This is why flash dryers and conveyor dryers are standard equipment in commercial shops. The heat polymerizes the ink—a chemical transformation that makes it wash-fast and long-lasting.

The industry adopted plastisol in the 1970s, and it became the gold standard for good reason. It's forgiving. Slightly thick application? No problem. Uneven pressure? The ink still flows reasonably well. Batch variations? Plastisol is consistent from batch to batch. For commercial screen printing shops cranking out high volumes, plastisol's reliability and speed made it indispensable.

Available Colors & Effects

Plastisol's dominance means manufacturers have developed virtually every color imaginable. Your ink supplier stocks hundreds of hues: spot colors, metallics, neons, pastels, earth tones. Beyond standard colors, plastisol supports special effects that water-based struggles with:

  • Puff Ink: Expands during cure, creating 3D texture. Popular for athletic wear and novelty designs.
  • High-Density Ink: Extra thick deposit for pronounced texture and detail.
  • Foil & Metallic Inks: Gold, silver, copper finishes that shine. Foil adheres to plastisol ink during curing.
  • Glow-in-the-Dark Ink: Contains phosphorescent particles that glow under UV light.
  • Glitter Ink: Suspended particles create sparkle. Durable under washing.

These specialty effects are what set screen printing apart from direct-to-garment or heat transfer. You can offer premium, textured, multi-sensory designs that digital methods can't replicate.

Curing & Performance

Proper curing temperature is non-negotiable: 320°F throughout the ink film. Under-cured prints fade, crack, and peel. Flash dryers briefly dry between colors. Conveyor dryers deliver full cure. Many shops verify cure with a temperature gun—if the ink surface reads below 320°F, it needs more time.

Properly cured plastisol prints deliver excellent durability. They withstand hundreds of washes, aggressive laundering, and commercial washing machines without fading or cracking. This is why athletic wear, workwear, and industrial apparel often use plastisol.

What is Water-Based Ink?

Water-based ink uses water as the primary solvent carrier, with pigments and binders dispersed throughout. When the water evaporates, the binders and pigments cure to the fabric. Unlike plastisol, water-based ink actively integrates into fabric fibers rather than sitting on top.

This distinction creates water-based's defining characteristic: an exceptionally soft hand-feel. Prints made with water-based ink feel like they're part of the garment, not a coating on top. Industry calls this "no hand"—you can barely feel the print. For fashion brands, premium retail apparel, and anything where comfort is a selling point, this quality is invaluable.

Water-based inks demand more technique. The water can evaporate while your screen is idle, drying the mesh and making future prints impossible. You need discipline: keeping screens wet, using retarders (chemicals that slow evaporation), and managing your pace. For high-volume production, this is a disadvantage. For boutique or custom printing, it's manageable and often the aesthetic trade-off is worth it.

Two Water-Based Subcategories

Water-based inks split into two approaches, each suited to different garment colors:

  • Standard Water-Based: Works well on light garments (white, pastels, light gray). The ink soaks in and the fibers transmit the color. On dark fabrics, the dark base shows through and mutes the color, resulting in dull, muddy prints.
  • Discharge Ink: A specialized water-based formulation that chemically bleaches the dye out of colored fabrics, then deposits new color in its place. Results on 100% cotton ring-spun fabric are stunning—vibrant, incredibly soft, vintage aesthetic.

Curing Water-Based

Curing water-based is more nuanced than plastisol. The process involves both evaporation and oxidation. Some premium water-based inks partially air-dry, though most require heating:

  1. Flash dryer to evaporate water (2-4 passes at lower temperature than plastisol)
  2. Conveyor dryer heat cure at 250-280°F (lower than plastisol's 320°F)
  3. Total cure time: 1-2 minutes in the dryer

Under-curing is the most common water-based mistake. Prints feel stiff when first cured, leading printers to assume they're done. In reality, the shirt needs time for oxidation to complete. Undercover cured water-based ink will crack and peel after a few washes.

Head-to-Head Comparison

Here's how the two inks stack up across the critical factors that impact your printing decisions and bottom line:

Feel / Hand

Plastisol

  • Noticeable layer on fabric
  • Can feel stiff, especially if thick
  • Premium when printed thin

Water-Based

  • Soft, no-hand feel
  • Part of the fabric, not a coating
  • Feels like natural fabric printing

Opacity on Dark Garments

Plastisol

  • Excellent opacity with white underbase
  • Opaque whites print clean over blacks
  • Multiple passes for intensity

Water-Based

  • Struggles on darks (unless discharge)
  • Dark fabric shows through color
  • Discharge is the solution

Color Vibrancy

Plastisol

  • Bright, saturated colors
  • Consistent color match
  • Stands out and pops

Water-Based

  • More muted, vintage aesthetic
  • Discharge offers vibrant colors on dark
  • Fashion-forward look

Durability & Wash-Fastness

Both inks, when properly cured, deliver excellent durability. Plastisol has a slight edge in extreme conditions (heavy industrial washing, commercial laundry). Water-based is completely adequate for retail and consumer apparel. Both easily survive 50+ home wash cycles without significant fading.

Screen Management

Plastisol

  • Won't dry in screens
  • Forgiving during long runs
  • Can pause and resume printing

Water-Based

  • Must keep screen wet
  • Use retarder to slow evaporation
  • Discipline required during breaks

Cleanup

Plastisol

  • Requires chemical solvents
  • Cost per cleanup: $2-5
  • Disposal regulations apply

Water-Based

  • Soap and water cleanup
  • Cost per cleanup: $0.50
  • Environmentally friendly

Environmental Impact

Water-based inks are significantly more eco-friendly. Plastisol contains PVC and phthalate plasticizers (though phthalate-free options are increasingly available). Water-based produces zero hazardous waste, uses water instead of solvents, and breaks down naturally. For brands pursuing OEKO-TEX or GOTS certification, water-based is essential.

Cost Comparison

Ink costs are comparable—both run $6-15 per pound. Water-based may require slightly more ink per garment (thinner film means some jobs need multiple passes). Setup and labor matter more than raw ink cost. Plastisol's faster production and forgiving technique often gives it an economic edge for high-volume work.

Learning Curve

Plastisol is beginner-friendly. Technique matters, but the ink forgives minor mistakes. Water-based demands precision: pressure, squeegee angle, screen management, cure verification. Many experienced printers find it harder to master water-based than to learn screen printing fundamentals. That said, the mastery is achievable and worth the effort.

When to Choose Plastisol

Plastisol remains the workhorse of screen printing. Choose plastisol when:

  • You're starting out: Plastisol is forgiving. You can focus on technique without fighting ink behavior.
  • High-volume production: Speed and consistency favor plastisol. Screens won't dry mid-run. You can pause and resume.
  • Any garment color: Darks, lights, and everything between. Plastisol works universally.
  • Bold, opaque designs: Logos, spot colors, vibrant graphics. Plastisol's opacity is unmatched.
  • Special effects: Puff ink, metallic, glitter, glow-in-dark—these are plastisol's domain.
  • Performance/athletic wear: Excellent durability under heavy washing and sweating.
  • Budget-conscious clients: Plastisol offers premium results at competitive pricing.
Pro Tip: Major ink suppliers like Wilflex, Union Ink, and Matsui offer plastisol formulations optimized for different fabric types and printing styles. Wilflex's PFP (plastisol for polyester) is specifically designed for synthetics, while standard formulations work best on cotton. Test inks with your typical garment bases to dial in the best results.

Most commercial screen printing shops remain plastisol-dominant. It's the industry standard for commercial work because it's proven, predictable, and profitable. If you're starting a shop and can only stock one ink type initially, plastisol is the smart choice. It opens doors to the broadest customer base.

When to Choose Water-Based

Water-based ink opens access to premium market segments increasingly willing to pay more for eco-conscious and soft-hand apparel:

  • Fashion brands: Retail and boutique clothing brands expect water-based or discharge prints. It's becoming table stakes.
  • Premium/luxury apparel: Bella+Canvas, Next Level, Comfort Colors, and other premium blanks pair beautifully with water-based inks.
  • Sustainability positioning: Brands pursuing eco-certifications (OEKO-TEX, GOTS, Fair Trade) need water-based to claim eco-friendly production.
  • Light garments with soft-hand requirement: Vintage aesthetic, soft feel are selling points.
  • Demonstrating capability: If you have water-based capability, you can bid jobs your competitor shops can't.
  • Higher profit margins: Water-based jobs often command 20-30% price premiums due to difficulty and perceived value.
Pro Tip: Brands like Rutland and Green Galaxy manufacture eco-certified water-based inks. These are more expensive but enable you to market your service as certified sustainable, opening doors to corporate sustainability programs and eco-conscious retailers willing to pay premium rates.

The water-based market is growing. Fashion designers, indie brands, and ethical manufacturers are increasingly specifying water-based or discharge. If your shop wants to serve retail and fashion brands, water-based isn't optional—it's a competitive requirement. Shops offering only plastisol are missing a high-margin market segment.

Discharge Ink: The Dark Horse

Discharge ink is a specialized subset of water-based that deserves its own discussion. It's not for every shop, but for those mastering it, discharge printing becomes a signature service and premium profit center.

How Discharge Works

Discharge ink contains an activator (typically a reducing agent) that chemically bleaches the dye out of colored fabric. Instead of covering the dark dye with ink (which creates muddy, low-contrast prints), discharge removes the color entirely in that area, then deposits a new color. The result: a print that looks genuinely integrated into the garment, not layered on top.

For a black t-shirt, imagine printing white discharge ink. The discharge activator bleaches out the black dye underneath, leaving white fibers. You deposit a new color (say, red) into those white areas. The result: a bright red print on a black shirt, no white underbase needed, and the print feels part of the fabric itself.

Requirements & Limitations

Discharge only works on 100% cotton ring-spun fabric. Blend fabrics don't work—synthetics don't bleach. The garment's dye chemistry matters enormously. Ring-spun cotton from consistent suppliers (Bella+Canvas, Next Level, American Apparel) produces reliable results. Cheap cotton blanks may have dyes that bleach unpredictably.

Discharge printing demands excellent environmental control. The activator produces formaldehyde gas during curing. You need strong ventilation, preferably a dedicated discharge station with exhaust capturing and venting outdoors. OSHA regulations apply. Proper training and equipment are non-negotiable.

Safety Warning: Discharge ink produces formaldehyde gas during heat curing. This is a health hazard. Proper ventilation is mandatory—you must capture and vent the discharge dryer exhaust outside, not recirculate it. Employees must be trained on safe handling. OSHA standards apply. If you're considering discharge printing, consult local environmental and occupational regulations before proceeding.

Why Invest in Discharge?

Because the print quality is unmatched. Colors pop. The vintage aesthetic is absolutely premium. Customers love the soft hand-feel and the visual quality. Discharge jobs command 50-100% price premiums compared to standard plastisol. A design that nets $2 in profit on a plastisol job might net $5-10 on discharge.

Top printers and shops positioning in the premium, fashion-forward market segment invest in discharge. It becomes a signature offering that differentiates your shop. Clients seeking discharge ink are quality-focused and price-insensitive—they're willing to pay for excellence.

Mixing Methods: Using Both in Your Shop

The smartest shops don't choose a side—they master both. Plastisol dominates the high-volume, commercial segment. Water-based and discharge own the premium, fashion, and sustainability niches. A well-rounded shop offers both, matching ink to client needs and design requirements.

Operational Setup

Using both inks requires careful operational management:

  • Separate screens: Plastisol and water-based residue contaminate each other. Designate screens exclusively to one type. Label them clearly.
  • Dedicated cleanup stations: Plastisol cleanup uses solvents. Water-based uses water. Keep them separate to prevent cross-contamination.
  • Different squeegees: Water-based can wear squeegees faster. Have dedicated squeegees for each ink type.
  • Training & documentation: Your production team must understand the differences. Document exposure times, curing temps, retarder use, and screen management for each ink type.
  • Inventory management: Stock inks separately. Clearly label. Train staff on proper storage (plastisol doesn't degrade, water-based has a limited shelf life).

Workflow Optimization

In practice, many shops batch-produce. Run high-volume plastisol jobs when you have multiple orders (economies of scale). Handle water-based and discharge jobs as custom/special orders, each uniquely priced at premium rates.

A typical week might look like: Monday-Wednesday, run commercial plastisol jobs (uniforms, promotional wear, bulk orders). Thursday-Friday, pivot to custom water-based and discharge orders (fashion clients, boutique brands, high-margin custom work). This workflow maximizes equipment utilization and keeps both ink systems active and practiced.

Competitive Advantage

Offering both inks makes you more competitive. Customers can choose based on their needs and budget. Fashion brands see a shop that understands their market. Commercial clients appreciate the versatility. You capture market share across segments, stabilizing revenue and increasing resilience against industry downturns.

Environmental Considerations

Sustainability matters increasingly to consumers, brands, and regulations. This is where water-based and discharge printing have a significant advantage.

Water-Based Environmental Profile

Water-based inks are dramatically more eco-friendly than plastisol. They contain no PVC, no phthalates, no persistent organic pollutants. Cleanup uses soap and water, not hazardous solvents. The inks biodegrade naturally. Waste water (if any) is non-toxic. Manufacturers like Green Galaxy and Rutland have achieved OEKO-TEX certification, meaning third parties have verified their inks meet strict environmental and health standards.

Plastisol's Evolution

The plastisol industry isn't ignoring environmental concerns. Phthalate-free plastisol formulations are now mainstream. PVC recycling programs exist, though still limited. Some manufacturers are exploring bio-based plasticizers as alternatives to PVC. The industry is evolving, but plastisol will always contain synthetic polymer components. It's not going zero-impact.

Certifications & Standards

If your clients pursue OEKO-TEX (textile safety standard), GOTS (organic cotton standard), or Fair Trade certification, you'll need water-based capability. These certifications explicitly require sustainable, non-toxic inks. Offering water-based and discharge inks positions your shop as sustainability-aligned, opening contracts with conscious brands.

The Honest Truth

No screen printing is zero-impact. Water-based is dramatically more sustainable than plastisol, but it's not perfect. The water used in cleanup still requires treatment. Pigments and binders, while natural, still have upstream environmental costs. The honest positioning: water-based is the responsible choice for apparel that values sustainability, while acknowledging that screen printing itself has environmental footprint. Improving process efficiency, minimizing waste, and choosing eco-conscious inks demonstrates commitment to reducing impact.

Making Your Decision

So which ink should you choose? The answer depends on your circumstances, ambitions, and market positioning.

If You're Starting Out

Start with plastisol. It's forgiving, widely available, and teaches you fundamentals without fighting ink behavior. You'll focus on technique, registration, curing, and getting comfortable with the process. Once you're reliably producing quality prints, expand into water-based. The transition is much easier once you've mastered plastisol basics.

If You're Scaling a Shop

Build capability in both. Plastisol becomes your bread-and-butter commercial work. Water-based and discharge become your profit margin multipliers. Offer both, price accordingly, and serve broader market segments. Shops offering only one ink type leave money on the table.

If You're Targeting Specific Markets

Fashion and retail brands expect water-based or discharge. Corporate apparel and uniforms suit plastisol. Sustainability-focused brands require water-based. Analyze your target customers and stock accordingly. If you're unsure, invest in water-based first—it's the growth area and opens premium market segments.

Continuous Learning

The screen printing industry evolves constantly. New inks, formulations, and techniques emerge regularly. Stay current:

  • Join ASPA for industry conferences, publications, and training resources
  • Experiment with new ink brands and formulations
  • Connect with other printers in the ASPA Member Directory for advice and best practices
  • Read industry publications and follow established printers on social media
  • Don't hesitate to ask colleagues questions—the screen printing community is collaborative
Pro Tip: Consider pursuing ASPA's Certified Screen Printer (CSP) credential. The exam covers ink chemistry, including plastisol vs. water-based applications and best practices. Certification demonstrates to clients and employers that you're serious about mastery. Many CSP holders report 15-20% higher pricing power and better client relationships.

Your Next Step

Whether you're starting your first screen printing setup or scaling an established shop, understand your customer base and market positioning. Plastisol and water-based are complementary, not competing. The most successful shops master both and use each strategically. Start where you are, scale thoughtfully, and never stop learning. The screen printing industry rewards shops that embrace versatility and commitment to craft.
