What is Sublimation Printing? Complete Guide to Dye Sublimation

Updated March 2026 · 12 min read

If you're exploring the garment decoration world, you'll keep hearing about sublimation. It's not screen printing—but it's a powerful complementary method that many screen printing shops add to their services. This guide explains what sublimation is, how it works, where it excels, and where it falls short.

Introduction: The Other Printing Method

Screen printing gets most of the spotlight in the apparel decoration world, but sublimation is quietly powering a massive segment of the industry. From esports team jerseys to personalized mugs in print-on-demand shops, sublimation delivers results that screen printing simply cannot match in certain niches.

The sublimation process works through a different chemistry than screen printing. While screen printing deposits ink on top of fabric, sublimation actually transforms fabric at the molecular level. The result? Prints with zero texture, unlimited colors, and exceptional durability.

This guide covers the complete sublimation story: what it is, how the science works, what equipment you need, where it excels, and most importantly—where it doesn't. By the end, you'll understand whether sublimation makes sense for your business or if it complements your existing screen printing operation.

How Sublimation Works: The Science

Sublimation is a chemical process where a solid transforms directly into a gas, bypassing the liquid state entirely. In sublimation printing, special inks exploit this property to bond permanently with polyester fabric at the molecular level.

The Process Step-by-Step

Here's what happens when you sublimate a design:

  1. Design to Transfer: You create artwork and print it using a sublimation printer onto special sublimation transfer paper. The ink sits on the surface of the paper, looking vibrant and glossy.
  2. Heat & Pressure: The transfer paper is placed face-down on your substrate (polyester shirt, mug, mouspad) and fed into a heat press set to approximately 400°F under pressure for 30-60 seconds depending on the substrate.
  3. Molecular Transformation: At that extreme temperature, the sublimation ink converts from a solid to a gas without ever becoming liquid. This gas penetrates the polyester fibers and bonds with them chemically.
  4. Cooling & Permanence: As the substrate cools, the gas solidifies back into a dye state, but now it's locked inside the fibers themselves—not sitting on top like screen printed ink.

Why Polyester Matters

Sublimation only works with polyester (or polyester-coated hard goods) because of how polyester's molecular structure works. When heated above 390°F, polyester fibers actually expand slightly, creating gaps that allow the sublimation dye to penetrate. Cotton and natural fabrics don't have this property, making sublimation impossible on them.

Think of it this way: sublimation is dyeing specific areas of the fabric in full color. The dye is literally part of the material. Once it cools, it can never wash out because it's chemically bonded—not physically deposited.

Pro Tip: Sublimation is a dye process, not an ink deposit. This is why sublimation prints have zero texture and feel exactly like the fabric beneath. There's no ink layer sitting on top—it's integrated into the fibers.

Equipment You Need

The startup cost for sublimation is lower than many people expect, and the equipment is straightforward. Let's break down each component you'll need.

Sublimation Printer

You need a printer specifically modified or designed for sublimation ink. Options:

  • Converted Epson Printers: The most affordable route. Popular models include the EcoTank ET-2850 and ET-15000. These are standard inkjet printers converted with sublimation cartridges. Cost: $250-$500. Perfect for beginners and small operations.
  • Dedicated Sublimation Printers: Purpose-built machines like the Sawgrass SG500 or SG1000 offer better color accuracy and longevity. Cost: $400-$800+. Worth the investment if you're printing seriously.

Critical: You cannot use regular inkjet ink for sublimation. Sublimation ink is specially formulated and costs more, but it's the only ink that will sublimate properly.

Heat Press

Your heat press is where the magic happens. Types:

  • Flat Press (for textiles): Presses flat items like t-shirts and fabric blanks. Minimum 15x15 inches recommended. Cost: $200-$600.
  • Mug Press: Specifically designed for cylindrical mugs and drinkware. Holds the mug in place for even heat distribution. Cost: $150-$400.
  • Cap Press: Specialized for baseball caps and hats. Cost: $300-$800.
  • Multi-Function Press: Some units swap heads, allowing you to handle multiple substrate types. Cost: $400-$1,000.

Sublimation Ink

Sublimation inks are the lifeblood of the process. Unlike screen printing inks, sublimation inks are formulated to sublimate at 400°F. Expect to pay $8-$20 per color (CMYK set: $30-$60). Quality matters here—cheap sublimation ink produces dull colors and poor saturation.

Sublimation Transfer Paper

This is specially coated paper that holds sublimation ink temporarily before transferring it to fabric. Standard letter-size sheets cost $0.20-$0.40 per sheet. A 100-sheet pack runs $20-$40. Never try to use regular paper—the coating is critical for proper ink transfer.

Substrates (What You Print On)

  • Polyester garments: 100% polyester t-shirts, jerseys, hoodies, athletic wear. Cost: $2-$8 per blank depending on quality.
  • Poly-coated hard goods: Mugs (ceramic with polyester coating), mousepads, phone cases, tiles, keychains, ornaments, license plates. Cost: $1-$20 each depending on item.

Startup Budget: Basic Sublimation Setup

  • Converted Epson Printer + Ink $250–$500
  • Flat Heat Press (15x15") $200–$400
  • Sublimation Ink & Transfer Paper $50–$100
  • Starter Blanks & Supplies $50–$150
  • Total Startup $550–$1,150

This is significantly cheaper than a screen printing startup. You can get sublimation running for under $1,500 with quality equipment. Compare that to a full screen printing setup ($2,000-$5,000+) and sublimation looks very attractive.

What You Can (and Can't) Sublimate

This is the critical section. Understanding what sublimation can and cannot do will determine whether it fits your business model.

What You CAN Sublimate

  • 100% Polyester Fabric: Any color works, but prints show best on WHITE or light polyester. Bold colors and dark shades work fine too.
  • Polyester Blends: 65%+ polyester blends work reasonably well. Lower ratios (50/50 cotton-poly) will have reduced vibrancy.
  • Poly-Coated Hard Goods: Mugs, tiles, ornaments, mousepads, coasters, phone cases, water bottles, keychains, license plates, awards, plaques, sublimation blanks in virtually any shape.
  • Athletic & Performance Wear: Moisture-wicking polyester shirts, jerseys, and athletic gear are IDEAL for sublimation.

What You CANNOT Sublimate

  • Cotton: Sublimation dye won't bond to cotton. The design will wash out completely. Full stop—no workarounds.
  • Dark-Colored Polyester: Sublimation inks are transparent/translucent. There's no white ink, so printing on dark polyester results in dull, muddy colors. You need a white or light base.
  • Natural Fibers: Wool, silk, linen, etc. won't work. Sublimation requires polyester's specific molecular properties.
  • Uncoated Metals/Glass/Ceramics: Bare ceramics, uncoated glass, and raw metals won't accept sublimation. They must be polyester-coated specifically for sublimation.
Critical Limitation: If you want to print on cotton t-shirts, sublimation is not the answer. This is THE deal-breaker for many people. If your market is 100% cotton apparel, skip sublimation entirely. Screen printing is the correct method for cotton.

Pros of Sublimation: Where It Excels

Sublimation isn't right for everyone, but where it fits, it delivers exceptional results. Here's why successful shops love it.

Zero Hand Feel

The print IS part of the fabric. There's no texture, no raised ink, no cracking. Run your hand over a sublimated print and it feels exactly like unprinted fabric. For athletic wear and high-end apparel, this is a massive advantage over screen printing's texture.

Full-Bleed & All-Over Printing

Unlike screen printing (which is limited by screen size), sublimation can print edge-to-edge and cover the entire garment. Want a design that wraps all the way around a sleeve? Sublimation handles it without limitations.

Unlimited Colors at No Extra Cost

Screen printing charges per color. A 4-color design requires 4 screens and 4 print strokes. Sublimation prints full CMYK process—unlimited colors in a single pass. A photographic image with 1,000 color variations costs the same as a solid color design.

Exceptional Durability

The dye is chemically bonded to the fiber. It won't crack, peel, fade (excessively), or wash out. Proper sublimation prints survive 50+ washes without visible degradation. This equals customer satisfaction and repeat business.

Low Per-Unit Cost

After the equipment investment, the cost per print is incredibly low. A shirt-sized transfer uses maybe $0.50-$1.00 in sublimation ink. Add transfer paper and blank cost, and you're at $1-$3 all-in per garment. Profit margins are excellent, especially on high-volume runs.

No Minimums

You can sublimate one item profitably. No screen setup cost, no color charges, no minimum order requirements. Perfect for print-on-demand and custom orders.

Diverse Product Range

The same printer handles shirts, mugs, mousepads, phone cases, tiles, ornaments, and more. One piece of equipment can service multiple product categories. Screen printing presses only print fabric.

Cons of Sublimation: Honest Limitations

Sublimation isn't perfect. These limitations are real and will affect your decision to invest.

Polyester Only (Or Poly-Coated)

This is the biggest limitation. If your customers want 100% cotton t-shirts, sublimation won't work. Many custom apparel buyers specifically request cotton, which locks you into screen printing or DTG methods.

Light Substrates Only

Dark colors and black polyester don't work. The transparent dye looks dull on dark fabric. You're limited to white and light-colored blanks for best results. This eliminates a whole market segment.

Color Shift Risk

Sublimation requires precise calibration. If your heat press temperature is off by 10-15°F, colors shift. If pressure is uneven, colors vary across the print. This requires learning ICC color profiles and testing extensively.

No Specialty Effects

You cannot do puff ink, metallic foil, glitter, glow-in-the-dark, or raised texture effects. Sublimation is flat color only. If customers want specialty effects, you'll need additional equipment or partner with other services.

Printer Maintenance

Sublimation printers are sensitive. If you don't print at least weekly, the print heads dry out and clog. Fixing clogs is time-consuming and expensive. You need regular use to keep the printer healthy. This isn't a "turn it on when you need it" tool.

Transfer Paper Waste

If you make a mistake or a print fails, you've wasted transfer paper and sublimation ink. You can't reprint on the same sheet. Screen printing lets you reuse screens; sublimation doesn't.

Learning Curve for Color Accuracy

Getting colors to match screen perfectly requires understanding sublimation chemistry, ICC profiles, and printer calibration. The learning curve is steeper than screen printing's "load ink, print" simplicity.

Sublimation vs Screen Printing: When to Use Each

The best answer isn't "which is better"—it's "which is right for this job?" Most successful shops use BOTH, understanding each method's strengths.

Volume & Economics

  • Small Volume (1-12 items): Sublimation wins. No screen setup, no color charges, profitable at low volume.
  • Medium Volume (12-50 items): Sublimation still wins unless you need cotton or special effects.
  • Large Volume (50+ items): Screen printing becomes competitive or cheaper. Faster prints, no transfer paper waste, proven at scale.

Fabric & Substrates

  • Cotton t-shirts: Screen printing only. Sublimation won't work.
  • Polyester athletic wear: Sublimation preferred. Better feel, unlimited colors, full-bleed capability.
  • Hard goods (mugs, tiles, etc.): Sublimation only. Screen printing can't handle hard surfaces.
  • Dark fabric: Screen printing. Sublimation produces dull results on dark colors.

Design Complexity

  • Bold, solid colors (1-4 colors): Screen printing is faster and cheaper per unit.
  • Photographic or multi-color designs: Sublimation wins. Unlimited colors at no extra cost.
  • Specialty effects (puff, foil, metallic): Screen printing offers options sublimation cannot.

Durability & Feel

  • Flat, soft feel required: Sublimation. Screen printing has texture.
  • Heavy-duty wear (work wear, industrial): Both are durable. Screen printing may be more robust for extreme conditions.
Best Practice: If you're a screen printing shop, adding a sublimation printer opens up product categories your press cannot touch—mugs alone can be a significant revenue stream. Think of sublimation as a COMPLEMENT to screen printing, not a replacement.

Business Applications: Where Sublimation Makes Money

Understanding where sublimation creates profit is key to deciding if it's right for you. Here are the most successful applications.

Esports & Gaming Jerseys

Esports teams need custom polyester performance jerseys with full-bleed, all-over designs. Sublimation is perfect—players want the light feel, teams want unlimited color designs, and the no-texture fit is ideal for competitive wear. This is a growth market.

Sportswear & Athletic Apparel

Moisture-wicking polyester is already the industry standard for athletes. Sublimation adds value by enabling custom designs without adding weight or texture. Soccer jerseys, volleyball uniforms, running shirts—all ideal sublimation candidates.

Promotional Products

Personalized mugs, mousepads, keychains, phone cases, and branded items are a huge market. Companies want custom branding without minimum orders. Sublimation's "one item profitably" model fits perfectly.

Awards & Recognition

Sublimation shines on plaques, trophies, name plates, medals, and awards. Custom photo-quality images are achievable cost-effectively. Schools and corporations love this.

Home Décor & Personalization

Polyester pillows, blankets, tiles, coasters, and wall décor benefit from custom photo sublimation. The market for personalized home items is exploding on Etsy and Shopify.

Print-On-Demand Services

Etsy sellers, Shopify stores, and print-on-demand platforms rely on sublimation's ability to handle single-item orders profitably. No minimums, no waste, full-bleed capability—perfect for e-commerce.

Profit Opportunity: If you're a screen printing shop, add a sublimation printer. The market for mugs alone is substantial—higher margin, lower volume dependency, and complements your existing screen work perfectly.

Getting Started with Sublimation

Ready to add sublimation to your operation? Here's a practical roadmap.

Step 1: Start with Affordable Equipment

Buy a converted Epson EcoTank printer ($250-$500), a basic flat heat press ($200-$400), and starter supplies. Total investment: under $1,200. This is genuinely affordable to test the market.

Step 2: Practice & Learn

Don't try to sell to customers immediately. Practice on cheap polyester blanks and mugs. Learn your equipment's quirks:

  • How hot does your press actually get? (temperature guns are essential)
  • What's the ideal pressure for even results?
  • How long should you press different substrates?
  • What does proper transfer look like?

Step 3: Master Color Management

Sublimation color accuracy requires ICC color profiles. These are calibration files that tell your printer how to reproduce colors accurately. Research your printer's profiles and learn how to use them. Many printer companies provide free profiles; use them.

Step 4: Join Communities & Learn

Join sublimation Facebook groups, watch YouTube tutorials from experienced printers, and connect with other sublimators. The community is collaborative and helpful.

Step 5: Test Products & Pricing

Start selling small batches of items—custom mugs, mousepads, personalized gifts. Get customer feedback. Adjust your pricing based on actual costs and market demand. Most shops find they can charge premium prices for personalized sublimated items.

Consider Sublimation as a Complement

If you already do screen printing, view sublimation as opening new revenue streams—mugs, hard goods, specialty items, and low-volume polyester orders that your screen press isn't ideal for. You're not replacing screen printing; you're expanding your service menu.

The Bottom Line

Sublimation printing is a powerful, versatile technology with a specific niche. It's not a screen printing replacement—it's a screen printing complement. And for certain applications, it's unbeatable.

Sublimation is right for you if:

  • You need to print on polyester or hard goods
  • Your customers want personalization or small-volume orders
  • You need all-over prints with unlimited colors
  • You want soft, flat prints without texture
  • You're operating print-on-demand or e-commerce

Skip sublimation if:

  • Your market is exclusively 100% cotton t-shirts
  • You only print bold, single-color designs
  • You need specialty effects (puff, foil, metallic)
  • You want high-volume production primarily

Many of the most successful custom apparel shops offer both screen printing AND sublimation. They understand that different methods serve different markets. A client needing 500 cotton t-shirts with a simple logo? Screen printing. A client needing 5 custom polyester esports jerseys? Sublimation. A client wanting personalized mugs? Sublimation.

If sublimation fits your market, the entry cost is reasonable, the learning curve is manageable, and the profit potential is real. Start small, test the market, and scale what works.
