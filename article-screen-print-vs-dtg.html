Screen Printing vs DTG vs Sublimation: Which Method is Right for You?

Updated March 2026 · 20 min read · By ASPA Editorial Team

The apparel decoration industry offers multiple printing methods, each with distinct advantages and limitations. Whether you're starting a print business, launching a merchandise line, or handling custom orders, understanding screen printing, DTG (direct-to-garment), and sublimation is essential. This guide compares all three methods across cost, quality, speed, durability, and practical use cases to help you choose the right approach for your specific needs.

Introduction: Choosing the Right Printing Method

The apparel decoration industry has evolved dramatically over the past two decades. What was once dominated entirely by screen printing now coexists with direct-to-garment (DTG) and sublimation technologies. Each method has proven itself valuable—but they excel in different scenarios.

The three dominant decoration methods serve different market segments and solve different business problems. Screen printing remains the industry standard for high-volume production with exceptional durability. DTG printing revolutionized small-batch and print-on-demand operations by eliminating setup costs. Sublimation captures the all-over-print and sportswear markets with vibrant, edge-to-edge results on polyester.

Your choice depends on several factors: order volume, fabric type, design complexity, budget constraints, turnaround time requirements, and your target market. This comprehensive guide walks through each method's strengths, limitations, costs, and ideal use cases—so you can make an informed decision aligned with your business goals.

Screen Printing: The Industry Standard

Screen printing is the oldest and most refined decoration method in the industry. A design is exposed onto a light-sensitive emulsion-coated mesh screen, and then ink is pushed through the openings onto fabric using a squeegee. Despite decades of technological innovation, screen printing remains the method of choice for production work because of its unmatched economics at volume and exceptional durability.

How Screen Printing Works

In screen printing, each color requires a separate screen. Your design is separated into different color layers (called color separations), and then each layer is exposed onto its own screen. During printing, the shirt moves through multiple print stations, receiving one color at a time, usually with flash-drying between colors to set the ink. The result: vibrant, thick, durable prints that last hundreds of washes.

Best For

  • Bulk orders: 24 or more units (the sweet spot is 50-500+)
  • Bold, solid designs: Logos, text, simple graphics
  • Athletic and performance wear: Screens printing delivers longevity
  • Prints on dark fabrics: Full vibrancy on blacks and navies without underbase needed

Pros of Screen Printing

  • Lowest per-unit cost at volume: After initial setup, the cost per shirt drops dramatically with quantity
  • Vibrant colors on darks: Screen printing produces brilliant colors on black and colored shirts
  • Exceptional durability: Properly cured plastisol prints outlast DTG and sublimation by far
  • Specialty effects: Only screen printing allows puff ink, metallic inks, foil effects, glow-in-the-dark, and discharge printing
  • Cost-effective at scale: Perfect for merch runs, team uniforms, and retail brands

Cons of Screen Printing

  • Setup cost per design: Each new design or color variation requires new screens
  • Not economical for one-offs: The setup cost makes small orders expensive per unit
  • Limited photographic detail: Reproducing photos requires halftone separation (advanced technique)
  • Turnaround time: Slower than DTG for design-to-delivery
  • Longer learning curve: Requires skill to master exposure, registration, and curing

Screen Printing Cost Breakdown

  • Setup Cost per Screen (1-4 color design) $25–$50 per screen
  • Per-Shirt Cost (24 units) $5–$7 each
  • Per-Shirt Cost (144 units) $2–$4 each
  • Per-Shirt Cost (500+ units) $1–$2 each
  • Blank T-Shirt (wholesale) $2–$4 each

The math is clear: screen printing's per-unit cost advantage grows with volume. A design that costs $10 total to print 24 shirts ($6 setup + $1.50 labor on cheap blanks) drops to $0.50 per shirt on a 500-unit run. This is why screen printing dominates production runs.

DTG (Direct-to-Garment): The Digital Revolution

Direct-to-garment printing brings digital printing technology directly to apparel. A DTG printer works like an industrial inkjet printer—it sprays colored inks directly onto fabric without screens, setup, or preparation. This revolutionary technology transformed print-on-demand and small-batch custom apparel by eliminating the economics of setup while delivering unlimited colors per print.

How DTG Works

DTG printers use specialized aqueous inks that require proper curing (heat) to set. Most DTG workflows include a pre-treatment step (applying a chemical to help ink adhere), then the digital image is printed directly onto the garment, and finally the shirt is heat-cured. The process is fast and requires no artwork preparation or color separation—you print what you design.

Best For

  • Short runs and one-offs: No minimums, no setup fees
  • Photo-realistic designs: Full-color artwork with gradients and photographic images
  • Print-on-demand businesses: Etsy, Shopify, and custom merchandise sites
  • Samples and proofs: Fast turnaround for design approval
  • Custom variable data: Each shirt can have different text or images (names, numbers, etc.)

Pros of DTG

  • No setup fees: Print one shirt or 1,000 for the same per-unit cost
  • Unlimited colors: Full-color artwork without color separation
  • Excellent for samples: Perfect for client proofs before screen printing production
  • Low minimum order: Ideal for e-commerce and custom gifting
  • Fast turnaround: Print-to-delivery in hours or days
  • No inventory pressure: Print only what sells (true print-on-demand)

Cons of DTG

  • Higher per-unit cost: $8–$20 per shirt regardless of quantity (vs. $1–$2 at volume in screen printing)
  • Slower production speed: Prints one shirt at a time (screen printing can run 10–100+ per minute)
  • Poor results on darks: Requires white underbase, which adds cost and can feel heavy on fabric
  • Cotton-only restriction: DTG works on 100% cotton or cotton-blend fabrics (not polyester or nylon)
  • Hand feel issues: Prints can feel thicker or stiffer than screen printing, especially with white underbase
  • Durability questions: Inks can fade faster than screen printing with heavy washing

DTG Printing Cost Breakdown

  • Per-Shirt Cost (any quantity) $8–$20 each
  • White Underbase (dark shirts) +$1–$3 per shirt
  • Pre-treatment Chemical +$0.50–$1 per shirt
  • Blank T-Shirt (wholesale) $2–$5 each
  • Equipment Cost (entry-level) $8,000–$15,000

DTG pricing is refreshingly simple: a flat per-shirt cost regardless of order size. This makes it ideal for businesses selling 1-10 units at a time (e-commerce, custom gifts) but economically uncompetitive when printing 50+ shirts of the same design.

Sublimation: Full-Coverage Color

Sublimation is a heat-transfer process where solid dyes become gaseous under heat and permanently bond with polyester fibers. The result is vibrant, edge-to-edge color with no texture or hand-feel issues. Sublimation dominates the all-over-print apparel market and hard goods (mugs, phone cases, mousepads) because it's the only method that achieves true full-bleed prints.

How Sublimation Works

First, the design is printed onto special sublimation transfer paper using sublimation inks. Then the transfer and garment are placed together and heated in a heat press to 390–410°F. Under heat, the dyes transform into gas that penetrates and bonds with polyester fibers permanently. Once cooled, the transfer is removed and the design remains. The result: no texture, vivid colors, and permanent durability.

Best For

  • All-over prints: Edge-to-edge designs without white margins
  • Polyester and poly-blend garments: Sportswear, performance wear, esports jerseys
  • Vibrant, full-color designs: Artwork with complex gradients and photographic images
  • Hard goods: Mugs, mousepads, phone cases, blankets, and promotional products
  • Corporate promotional items: Branded merchandise with full-color logos

Pros of Sublimation

  • No texture or hand feel: Prints feel like part of the fabric (not on top of it)
  • Edge-to-edge printing: Designs can run all the way to the edges (true full-bleed)
  • Vibrant colors: Dyes bond permanently with polyester for bright, fade-resistant results
  • Exceptional durability: Colors last as long as the garment (never crack, peel, or fade)
  • Versatility: Works on apparel and hard goods (mugs, hats, blankets, etc.)

Cons of Sublimation

  • Polyester-only: Does NOT work on 100% cotton (the biggest limitation)
  • Light colors only: White and light-colored garments only (dyes won't show on dark fabrics)
  • Higher equipment cost: Heat press setup is expensive ($1,500–$3,000+)
  • Transfer paper waste: Leftover transfer paper is not reusable
  • Learning curve: Temperature control and curing time are critical
  • Bleed management: Mistakes in transfer placement can't be fixed

Sublimation Printing Cost Breakdown

  • Per-Shirt Cost (light coverage) $5–$8 each
  • Per-Shirt Cost (full coverage) $8–$15 each
  • Transfer Paper $0.50–$1 per shirt
  • Sublimation Inks $30–$50 per bottle
  • Heat Press Equipment $1,500–$3,000

Sublimation's per-unit cost scales with print coverage. A small logo on a 100% poly shirt costs $5–$8, while a full-coverage all-over print runs $10–$15. This makes sublimation economical for higher-value products where customers expect premium quality.

Head-to-Head Comparison

To make the best choice, you need to understand how these three methods compare across critical metrics. Below is a detailed comparison covering the factors that matter most for your decision.

Print Quality & Color Vibrancy

Screen Printing

On Light Fabric: Excellent; vibrant colors, opaque
On Dark Fabric: Excellent; best choice for darks
Photorealistic: Limited (requires halftone separation)

DTG Printing

On Light Fabric: Excellent; full-color photorealistic
On Dark Fabric: Fair; requires white underbase (adds cost)
Photorealistic: Excellent; perfect for photos and gradients

Sublimation

On Light Fabric: Excellent; vibrant dyes, permanent
On Dark Fabric: Not possible; only works on light polyester
Photorealistic: Excellent; gradients and photos shine

Durability & Wash Fastness

Screen Printing

Wash Fastness: 50+ washes, minimal fading
Cracking/Peeling: Extremely rare if cured properly
Best For: Apparel that will be worn frequently

DTG Printing

Wash Fastness: 20–30 washes, some fading
Cracking/Peeling: Can crack if white underbase used heavily
Best For: Casual wear, collectibles, low-wash items

Sublimation

Wash Fastness: 100+ washes, no fading
Cracking/Peeling: Impossible (dyes are part of fiber)
Best For: Performance wear, high-wash items

Fabric Compatibility

Screen Printing

Cotton: Perfect; best results
Polyester: Good; works well
Blends: Excellent; most versatile
Nylon/Other: Yes; works on most fabrics

DTG Printing

Cotton: Perfect; ideal choice
Polyester: Poor; ink doesn't adhere well
Blends: Fair; 50% cotton+ works
Nylon/Other: No; cotton-only technology

Sublimation

Cotton: No; dyes won't bond
Polyester: Perfect; designed for poly
Blends: Good; 65%+ poly recommended
Hard Goods: Yes; mugs, mousepads, etc.

Minimum Order & Per-Unit Cost at Scale

Screen Printing

Minimum Order: 1–24 units (but not economical under 24)
1 Shirt: $30–$50 total (not recommended)
24 Shirts: $5–$7 each
500 Shirts: $1–$2 each

DTG Printing

Minimum Order: 1 unit (true one-off capable)
1 Shirt: $10–$20 each
24 Shirts: $8–$15 each
500 Shirts: $8–$15 each (no volume discount)

Sublimation

Minimum Order: 1 unit (per-item pricing)
1 Shirt: $8–$15 each
24 Shirts: $8–$12 each
500 Shirts: $6–$10 each (modest volume discount)

Turnaround Time

Screen Printing

Setup Time: 1–2 days (screen exposure, registration)
Production Time: 2–5 business days (depending on volume)
Fastest Option?: No; slower than DTG

DTG Printing

Setup Time: Minimal; just load the file
Production Time: Same day or next day (print-to-ship)
Fastest Option?: Yes; fastest for small orders

Sublimation

Setup Time: Minimal; prepare transfer and garment
Production Time: 1–3 days (heat press cycle time)
Fastest Option?: Moderate; faster than screen printing
Pro Tip: For most businesses, the best approach is understanding your volume and design type. 50+ units of simple designs? Screen printing wins on cost. 1–24 units of complex art? DTG is unbeatable. All-over sportswear designs? Sublimation owns that space.

When to Choose Screen Printing

Screen printing remains the default choice for production work and bulk orders. If your business model involves printing the same (or similar) designs repeatedly, screen printing's unit economics are unbeatable. Here are the scenarios where screen printing is the clear winner.

Team Uniforms & Athletic Programs

A high school needs 150 volleyball uniforms with the same logo and team name. Screen printing shines here. The cost for 150 shirts: approximately $225–$300 (at $1.50–$2 per shirt), compared to $1,200–$2,250 for DTG. The difference: screen printing saves $900+ on this single order. These are real, significant savings that make or break the business case for schools and athletic programs.

Merchandise Runs & Retail Brands

A streetwear brand produces a limited edition drop of 500 hoodies with a custom graphic. With screen printing, the per-unit cost is $1.50–$2.50. The same order via DTG would cost $10–$15 per unit—a $4,000–$6,500 difference on margin. For brands with multiple product lines, this scale advantage is why they use screen printing almost exclusively.

Promotional Giveaways

A corporation orders 1,000 branded t-shirts for a conference. Screen printing at $1–$1.50 per shirt costs $1,000–$1,500. DTG would cost $8,000–$15,000 for the same order. The decision is obvious.

Specialty Effects

Only screen printing allows certain effects: puff ink (3D texture), foil (metallic shine), glow-in-the-dark, metallic inks (gold, silver, copper), and discharge printing (removing dye from colored fabric). If your design requires any of these effects, screen printing is your only choice.

The Math of Screen Printing Economics

Screen printing's cost advantage appears at volume. Here's a realistic scenario:

  • 24-unit order: Screen printing ~$144 (setup + printing) vs DTG ~$240. Screen printing saves $96.
  • 144-unit order: Screen printing ~$432 (setup + printing) vs DTG ~$1,440. Screen printing saves $1,008.
  • 500-unit order: Screen printing ~$750 (setup + printing) vs DTG ~$5,000. Screen printing saves $4,250.

The breakeven point is roughly 20–24 units. Below that, DTG is often competitive. Above that, screen printing's economics are overwhelming.

Pro Tip: Many shops combine methods strategically. Screen print the main design for the bulk of an order, then DTG custom variable data (names, numbers, custom requests) on top. This hybrid approach captures the cost savings of screen printing while offering customization.

When to Choose DTG

DTG revolutionized the apparel business by eliminating the "minimum order" problem. If your model involves printing small quantities, unique designs, or one-off custom orders, DTG is your answer. The technology is perfect for e-commerce, print-on-demand, and custom gifting businesses.

Print-on-Demand Businesses

An Etsy seller prints custom t-shirts: one order for a personalized gift, another for a niche fandom design, a third for a local band. Each order is unique. DTG eliminates setup costs and minimum orders, allowing the seller to operate a true made-to-order business. Without DTG (or sublimation), this entire business model doesn't exist.

Shopify / E-Commerce Storefronts

A Shopify store sells 5–20 t-shirts per week in different designs. Screen printing requires 24+ unit minimums, making small e-commerce uneconomical. DTG allows the store to fulfill orders as they come in, holding no inventory. The customer pays $35–$50 for a custom shirt; the printer spends $10–$15 in DTG costs, leaving healthy margin without holding inventory risk.

Prototyping & Design Approval

A large brand's design team creates a sample for client approval. Instead of committing to 24-unit screen printing minimums, they order 2–3 DTG samples for quick proofing. Once approved, the full production order goes to screen printing. DTG handles the risky, exploratory phase; screen printing handles the profitable production phase.

Photo-Based Designs

A wedding photographer offers custom t-shirts with wedding photos. Each design is unique (different photos for different weddings). DTG's unlimited colors and photorealistic capability are perfect. Screen printing's halftone process would lose detail and require manual color separation for each unique image—not practical.

Custom Event Merchandise

A local band records a concert and wants to sell limited-edition merchandise. DTG allows them to offer 50 unique designs (different concert photos, setlists, etc.) without committing to 24+ of each design. If a design sells 8 units and another sells 2, DTG accommodates both without waste.

The DTG Economics Sweet Spot

DTG's flat per-unit pricing ($8–$20 per shirt, regardless of quantity) is optimal for:

  • Orders under 24 units (where screen printing's setup cost isn't recovered)
  • Highly variable designs (each shirt different)
  • High-value custom products (where per-unit cost is less important than uniqueness)
  • Businesses with capital constraints (no large equipment investment needed)

DTG's weakness is volume. Printing 500 identical shirts via DTG costs $4,000–$7,500; screen printing costs $500–$1,000. For production runs, screen printing dominates.

When to Choose Sublimation

Sublimation owns specific niches where its advantages (edge-to-edge printing, soft hand-feel, no texture, vibrant dyes) are essential. If your product is polyester or you need all-over prints without margins, sublimation is often the only practical option.

All-Over Print Apparel

A streetwear brand designs a vibrant psychedelic pattern that covers the entire shirt from collar to hem, front and back. Sublimation's edge-to-edge capability allows designs to run all the way to the seams without white margins. Screen printing would require multiple screens and registration adjustments for near-full coverage; sublimation achieves true full-bleed easily. The result looks premium, feels soft, and costs $10–$15 per shirt.

Sportswear & Performance Wear

A company manufactures moisture-wicking esports jerseys in polyester. Sublimation's soft hand-feel (no texture on the print), edge-to-edge capability, and exceptional wash-fastness are ideal. Players appreciate the comfort, and sponsors love the premium look. Sublimation is the standard in professional esports, sports apparel, and athletic wear.

Hard Goods & Promotional Products

A promotional company wants to sell custom mugs, mousepads, phone cases, and blankets. Sublimation is the only method that works on these items. A polyester blanket with a vibrant full-bleed design is achievable only through sublimation. The economics work: each blank costs $5–$10, sublimation adds $3–$8, and retail price is $25–$40. Profit margin is healthy, and the product quality is premium.

Corporate Branded Items

A Fortune 500 company orders 5,000 polyester branded hoodies for employee uniforms. Sublimation produces vibrant, edge-to-edge logos without texture. The soft hand-feel and premium appearance justify the cost ($8–$12 per unit). The perceived quality is high, and the durability is exceptional.

Fashion & Design-Focused Brands

A designer brand sells limited-edition all-over-print polyester shirts at premium prices ($80–$150 retail). Sublimation's soft hand-feel, vibrant colors, and premium appearance justify the price point. Customers expect a certain level of quality; sublimation delivers it.

The Sublimation Constraint & Opportunity

Sublimation's limitation (polyester-only) is simultaneously its opportunity. In the market segments that use polyester (sportswear, performance wear, all-over print fashion, hard goods), sublimation has minimal competition. Screen printing and DTG don't work well on polyester (or don't work at all), making sublimation the default technology in these niches.

Pro Tip: Look for 65–85% polyester / 15–35% cotton blends. These "poly-blend sweet spots" work well with sublimation while maintaining a premium cotton feel. They're ideal for eco-conscious brands and performance apparel.

Can You Combine Methods?

The most versatile print shops and brands don't choose one method—they combine them strategically. Using multiple technologies allows businesses to serve diverse market segments and handle complex projects that no single method can address optimally.

Screen Print + DTG Hybrid Approach

A merchandise company takes a 100-shirt order: 98 identical designs + 2 custom one-offs. They screen print the 98 units for $2 each ($196 total), then DTG the 2 custom shirts for $15 each ($30 total). Total cost: $226. This hybrid approach captures screen printing's cost advantage while accommodating customization that DTG enables.

Screen Print Base + Custom Variable Data

A team orders 24 jerseys with the team logo (screen printed) but each jersey needs a custom name and number on the back. The shop screen prints the logo on all 24, then DTG prints the variable data (different names/numbers) on each one. Cost: screen print 24 logos at $3 each ($72) + DTG 24 variable prints at $5 each ($120) = $192 total. The result combines the cost efficiency of screen printing with the customization of DTG.

Sublimation + Screen Print Hybrid

A fashion brand designs a polyester all-over print shirt with metallic accents. Sublimation prints the base design (achieving edge-to-edge vibrant color), then screen printing adds metallic foil accents on top. The result is impossible with any single method: full-color edge-to-edge base + metallic shimmer accents. Cost is higher (~$15–$20 per unit), but perceived value is premium.

Multi-Method Shop Strategy

Professional print shops invest in screen printing equipment (capital intensive but produces best margins at volume), DTG equipment (serves small orders and e-commerce), and sometimes sublimation. They route each order to the most appropriate method:

  • Screen printing for 50+ unit bulk orders
  • DTG for 1–24 unit custom orders
  • Sublimation for polyester or all-over print work

This flexibility allows them to serve every market segment, maximize profit margins, and build strong customer relationships across different business types.

Being versatile is competitive. A shop that can only screen print loses DTG orders; one that only does DTG can't handle production runs. Multi-method shops are harder to displace and more resilient to market shifts.

Making Your Decision: A Practical Framework

You've learned about all three methods. Now, how do you choose? Use this decision framework based on your specific situation.

Ask Yourself These Questions:

  • What is the typical order volume? 1–24 units? DTG. 25–99? It depends. 100+? Screen printing usually wins.
  • What fabric are you printing on? Cotton? Screen printing or DTG. Polyester? Sublimation. Blends? Screen printing is most versatile.
  • Do designs repeat or vary? Same design many times? Screen printing. Every design different? DTG. All-over prints? Sublimation.
  • What is your margin requirement? High-margin, low-volume? DTG or sublimation. Low-margin, high-volume? Screen printing.
  • Do you need specialty effects? Puff, foil, metallic, discharge? Screen printing only.
  • What is your capital budget? Limited budget? DTG or sublimation ($8,000–$15,000). Larger budget? Screen printing offers best long-term ROI ($3,000–$10,000+ for a complete setup).
  • What is your turnaround requirement? Next-day shipping? DTG. A few days? Any method. A week? Screen printing is fine.

Quick Decision Logic:

  • IF: Bulk orders (50+), simple designs, cotton or blends, any color THEN: Screen printing
  • IF: Small orders (1–24), complex art, photo-realistic, fast turnaround THEN: DTG
  • IF: Polyester or poly-blends, all-over prints, sportswear, premium perceived value THEN: Sublimation
