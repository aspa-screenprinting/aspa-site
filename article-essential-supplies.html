Essential Supplies Every New Screen Printer Needs

Updated March 2026 · 14 min read

You've got your press. You've watched the videos. Now you need supplies—and the list is longer than most beginners expect. This guide covers every consumable, chemical, and tool you need to start printing, organized by category with specific product recommendations and approximate costs. Think of this as your shopping list before your first print.

Introduction: Your Supply Checklist Starts Here

Starting a screen printing operation requires more than just a press. Between consumable inks, emulsion chemicals, mesh screens, and specialized tools, you'll need to stock up on dozens of items before printing your first shirt. The good news is that most supplies are affordable, and smart planning means you won't waste money on duplicates or the wrong items.

This guide breaks down every essential supply category, explains what you actually need (versus nice-to-have), and gives you ballpark costs. We'll cover screens, inks, chemicals, tools, and where to find reliable suppliers. By the end, you'll have a complete shopping list and a clear budget for getting started.

Whether you're printing five shirts for your band or starting a small business, understanding your supply chain matters. You'll replenish consumables constantly, so building relationships with suppliers now saves headaches (and money) down the road.

Screens and Frames

Your screens are the foundation of screen printing. A screen is mesh stretched tightly over a frame, coated with emulsion, and exposed with your design. Getting this right means crisp prints; getting it wrong means frustration.

Pre-Stretched Aluminum Frames

Pre-stretched aluminum frames hold tension better and cost slightly more than wood frames—but for production work, they're essential. They maintain even tension over months of use and resist warping from moisture and temperature changes.

  • Cost: $20-40 per frame
  • Standard sizes: 20x24" is the all-purpose size for most garment printing
  • Why invest: Consistent tension means sharper prints and longer frame life

Wood Frames

Cheaper than aluminum, wood frames work fine for learning, but they have drawbacks. Wood loses tension quickly and warps with moisture—not ideal for production. If you're just experimenting, they're acceptable; if you're planning to print regularly, save the money and go aluminum.

  • Cost: $8-15 per frame
  • Best for: Quick tests and learning only

Mesh Counts to Stock

Different mesh counts (thread density per inch) are optimized for different work. Start with two primary counts and add others as you grow.

  • 110 Mesh: General-purpose, plastisol inks. Large holes allow more ink through. Perfect for bold text, solid colors, and simple graphics. About 80% of beginner work uses this.
  • 156 Mesh: The versatile middle ground. Finer detail than 110, but still pushes ink through easily. Works for both plastisol and water-based inks. Start with two of these.
  • 230 Mesh: Fine detail and halftone work. Used for photographic images and intricate artwork. Much tighter control, but slower to print.
  • 280+ Mesh: Ultra-fine specialty applications. Overkill for beginners—add later when you have specific need.

How Many Screens Do You Need?

Start with 6-10 screens for a small operation. You'll need multiples for multi-color jobs (each color gets its own screen) and while screens are being reclaimed for new designs. Having backup screens means you can keep printing while others are being cleaned.

Screens & Frames Budget

  • 6 pre-stretched aluminum frames (mixed mesh) $150-300

Inks

Ink is the star of screen printing—what your customers see and feel. There are two main types to understand, and starting with the right one matters.

Plastisol Ink: The Beginner's Best Friend

Plastisol is the industry standard for beginners and small shops. It's forgiving, doesn't dry in the screen (you can pause for hours and come back), and produces vibrant results.

  • How it works: Oil-based, cures through heat (not evaporation). Won't dry on screens, so you have flexibility.
  • Starter colors to buy: Black, white, red, royal blue, navy, gold/athletic gold. Start with quart sizes.
  • White ink is essential: You'll use more white than any other color (for underbase on dark shirts). Buy a full gallon of white immediately.
  • Brands to trust: Wilflex Epic (industry standard), Union Ink Ultrasoft, IC Ink
  • Cost: $6-15 per pound, typically $100-200 for a starter ink set

Mixing Systems vs. Ready-Made Colors

You can mix custom Pantone colors if you invest in a color mixing system (more advanced and expensive). For beginners, buy pre-mixed colors. They're consistent and eliminate guesswork.

Water-Based Inks: The Future (But Later)

Water-based inks are growing in popularity because they're eco-friendly and feel softer on fabric. But they require skill—they dry faster on screens, can be finicky, and cost more. Try them after you've mastered plastisol.

  • Brands: Green Galaxy, Matsui
  • When to switch: After you've printed 100+ shirts with plastisol and understand the fundamentals
Safety Note: Store inks properly. Plastisol inks can separate if stored too cold; keep in 60-80°F environment. Water-based inks can grow mold if left uncovered. Label everything and keep away from children and food.

Emulsion and Exposure Supplies

Emulsion is the light-sensitive chemical that makes screen printing possible. It hardens where light hits, creating a stencil. Getting quality emulsion and tools ensures your screens expose perfectly.

Emulsion Type: SBQ (Photopolymer)

SBQ photopolymer emulsion is recommended for beginners—pre-sensitized (no mixing required), consistent results, and predictable cure times.

  • Top brands: Baselayr Long Lasting, Chromaline ChromaBlue, Ulano QTX
  • Cost: $20-40 per gallon (makes 20+ screens)
  • Storage: Keep in cool, dark place. Emulsion has shelf life—use fresh supplies for best results.

Scoop Coater: Non-Negotiable

A scoop coater applies emulsion evenly to screens. It's simple but critical—a cheap, uneven coater ruins screens before you even expose them.

  • Must match screen width: Get a 16" coater for 20x24" screens
  • Cost: $15-30 for a quality one
  • Pro tip: Invest in a good coater—cheap ones have uneven edges that cause streaks

Transparency Film for Artwork

Your artwork gets printed onto waterproof transparency film, which acts as a stencil during exposure. The film must be opaque black—if you can see light through the black areas, it's not opaque enough.

  • Best choice: Waterproof inkjet transparency film
  • Cost: $20-30 for 100 sheets ($0.20-0.30 per sheet)
  • Critical rule: Must be OPAQUE BLACK. Hold up to light—if you see through it, it won't work

Exposure Unit

Emulsion hardens when exposed to UV light. You can use sunlight for free (inconsistent) or invest in a proper unit.

  • DIY Sun Exposure: Free, but weather-dependent and inconsistent. Works in a pinch.
  • UV Exposure Box: $400-2,000. Professional, consistent, reliable for serious work.

Emulsion & Exposure Budget

  • Emulsion (1 gallon) $20-40
  • Scoop coater $15-30
  • Transparency film (100 sheets) $20-30
  • Total supplies (no unit) $55-100

Chemicals and Cleaners

These are the unglamorous consumables you'll go through constantly. They're inexpensive individually but add up over time. Quality matters—cheap chemicals can damage screens and waste your time.

Screen Degreaser

Used before coating every screen with emulsion. New aluminum frames come with oils that block emulsion adhesion. You must degrease first.

  • Cost: $10-20 per quart
  • Brands: Franmar Bean-E-Doo (eco-friendly), CCI

Emulsion Remover/Reclaimer

Strips old emulsion so you can reuse screens. Essential if you're printing multiple designs on the same frame (which you should be).

  • Cost: $15-25 per quart
  • How to use: Apply, let sit 5-15 minutes, pressure wash away old emulsion

Haze Remover / Ghost Remover

For stubborn stains left after reclaiming. Not needed every time, but essential when standard reclaiming doesn't completely clean the screen.

  • Cost: $15-25 per quart

Spot Cleaner / Ink Degradent

Used to clean ink off screens during press runs when changing colors or fixing mistakes. Keeps screens open and prevents ink from drying.

  • Cost: $15-20 per spray bottle

Screen Opener

Keeps mesh open if ink starts to dry during printing (mainly for water-based inks). Prevents premature drying that can ruin a print run.

  • Cost: $10-15 per spray bottle
Warning: These chemicals are strong. Read all product labels and safety data sheets (SDS). Label everything clearly. Store away from children and food. Some require proper ventilation. Dispose responsibly—don't pour solvents down the drain.

Chemicals & Cleaners Budget

  • Screen degreaser (1 qt) $10-20
  • Emulsion remover (1 qt) $15-25
  • Haze remover (1 qt) $15-25
  • Spot cleaner/degradent $15-20
  • Screen opener $10-15
  • Total chemicals kit $75-130

Squeegees and Flood Bars

The squeegee is your most-used tool. It pushes ink through the screen onto the shirt. Quality and durometer (hardness) matter enormously—a bad squeegee ruins prints.

Squeegee Durometer

Durometer measures squeegee hardness on a scale of 0-100. Different durometers are optimized for different applications.

  • 60-70 Durometer (soft): Best for textile printing. Deposits more ink. Most versatile for t-shirts and fabrics. Start with 70.
  • 80-90 Durometer (hard): For hard substrates (plastics, rigid materials) or when you need thin ink deposit. More advanced.

Squeegee Size & Blade Shape

Size should be your print area plus 1" on each side. Most shirt prints fit within a 14" squeegee. The blade has three shapes: square edge (most common), sharp point (fine detail), and rounded edge (softer deposit).

  • Cost: $20-50 per squeegee
  • Start with: 14" squeegee, 70 durometer, square edge

Flood Bar

A flood bar fills the screen with ink before the print stroke. Some printers use the squeegee for both jobs; a dedicated flood bar gives better control. Softer rubber than squeegees.

  • Optional but helpful for consistent ink distribution

Squeegee Sharpener

Over time, squeegee edges round off and print quality suffers. A sharpener ($50-100) extends their life significantly by restoring sharp edges.

Squeegees Budget

  • Squeegee (14", 70 durometer) $20-50
  • Flood bar (optional) $10-20
  • Total startup squeegees $30-70

Blank Garments

The quality of your blank garment matters as much as the quality of your print. A perfect design on a cheap, scratchy shirt still feels cheap. Invest in quality blanks for production work.

Practice vs. Production Blanks

  • Practice blanks: Cheap cotton tees (Gildan 5000, Fruit of the Loom) for test prints. Budget $1-2 each.
  • Production blanks: Higher quality, consistent fit and feel. Build relationships with distributors for best pricing.

Quality Tiers

  • Gildan 5000: Budget option, basic quality. $2-3 each in bulk.
  • Next Level 3600: Mid-range, retail quality. $3-4 each.
  • Bella+Canvas 3001: Premium soft tee. $4-5 each. Best-in-class feel and fit.

Where to Buy: The Big Three Distributors

SanMar, S&S Activewear, and Alphabroder are the industry's largest blank distributors. All require a wholesale account (simple to set up with business license/resale certificate). Bulk pricing is dramatically better than retail.

  • Buy by the case: Standard orders are 72 or 144 units per style. Pricing drops significantly.
  • Pro tip: Know shrinkage specs before buying. Pre-shrunk blanks are essential—avoid surprises.

Starter Budget for Practice

Budget $50-100 for practice blanks (25-50 cheap tees). Don't waste premium blanks while learning—use budget blanks for test prints.

Tools and Accessories

These are the small items that add up. None are expensive individually, but together they're essential for smooth operations.

Pallet Tape / Platen Adhesive

Keeps shirts flat on the platen during printing. Prevents shifting and wrinkles. Spray adhesive is quick; water-based pallet adhesive is more durable.

  • Spray adhesive: $8-12 per can
  • Pallet adhesive: $15-20 per bottle

Registration System

For multi-color prints, you need precise alignment of screens. Options: T-square guides, tri-loc tabs, or built-in registration on your press.

Screen Printing Tape

Blocks off areas on screens to prevent ink leaking. NOT regular masking tape (leaves residue). Use dedicated screen printing tape.

  • Cost: $5-10 per roll

Ink Tools

  • Spatulas/ink knives: For scooping and mixing ink. $5-10 per set.
  • Spray bottles: For water and emulsion remover application. $2-3 each.

Shop Towels

You'll go through tons of these. Industrial blue shop towels are best—cheap, absorbent, and durable. Buy the big rolls.

  • Cost: $15-20 per roll (lasts months)

Infrared Temperature Gun

Measures cure temperature without touching hot surfaces. Essential for verifying proper cure (must reach 320°F for plastisol).

  • Cost: $15-25
  • Why essential: You can't see proper curing—temperature gun is proof

Tools & Accessories Budget

  • Pallet adhesive/spray $10-20
  • Screen printing tape $5-10
  • Ink tools & brushes $10-15
  • Shop towels $15-20
  • Temperature gun $15-25
  • Total tools $55-90

Where to Buy: Supplier Guide

Finding reliable suppliers is as important as knowing what to buy. Establish relationships early—consistent supply chains beat hunting for bargains.

Online Specialty Retailers

These are ideal for beginners. Wide selection, competitive pricing, and often educational content on their websites.

  • ScreenPrintingSupply.com: Huge inventory, fast shipping, competitive prices
  • GoldUpUSA.com: Focused on supplies and equipment, good for finding specific items
  • Ryonet / ScreenPrinting.com: Excellent customer service, educational videos on their site

Major Distributors

Nazdar, PolyOne (Wilflex), and CCI are huge distributors. Better pricing on bulk orders, but typically require minimum orders.

Amazon

Fine for basics (transparencies, spray adhesive, temperature gun). Avoid buying inks and emulsion on Amazon unless from authorized sellers—counterfeits exist.

Local Screen Printing Supply Stores

If one exists in your area, visit. Hands-on help, no shipping costs, and you build relationships that matter.

Pro Tip: Build relationships with 1-2 reliable suppliers. Consistent supply chains matter more than saving $2 on ink. Plus, experienced suppliers offer advice and troubleshooting.

Your Starter Supply Budget

Here's a complete breakdown of what you'll spend on supplies to get started. Remember: this is SUPPLIES ONLY. Equipment (press, dryer, exposure unit) is separate.

Complete Startup Supplies Budget

  • Screens & frames (6-10) $150-300
  • Inks (starter set + white gallon) $100-200
  • Emulsion & exposure supplies $60-120
  • Chemicals & cleaners $75-130
  • Squeegees & flood bars $50-80
  • Practice blank garments $50-100
  • Tools & accessories $50-80
  • TOTAL SUPPLIES BUDGET $535-1,010

Ongoing Consumable Costs

Once you're up and running, expect monthly consumable costs:

  • Small operation (10-20 prints/month): $50-100/month for ink, emulsion, chemicals, blanks
  • Active operation (50-100 prints/month): $150-300/month
  • High volume (200+ prints/month): $400-800/month or higher

Build Supplier Relationships Now

Once you join ASPA, you gain access to member discounts from participating suppliers. This can save 10-20% on regular supplies—which adds up significantly over a year. Plus, you'll connect with other screen printers who share recommendations and insider tips.

Pro Tip: You'll replenish supplies constantly once you're printing regularly. Plan for $100-200/month in consumables going forward. Build this into your pricing so it's covered by your production revenue.
